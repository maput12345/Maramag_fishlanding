## CHAPTER II: REVIEW OF RELATED LITERATURE

### 1. Introduction

This chapter presents the related literature and studies relevant to the development of the Point-of-Sale (POS) and Inventory Management System for Maramag Fish Landing. It focuses on studies about digital inventory systems, POS-supported transaction processing, information and communication technology (ICT) adoption in small and medium-sized enterprises (SMEs), QR code-based traceability, and fishery-related monitoring systems. These works provide the conceptual and practical foundation for understanding how digital tools can improve efficiency, accuracy, and transparency in operational environments that still rely on manual procedures.

The review also includes local and international studies that discuss the common limitations of manual systems, such as delayed reporting, data inconsistency, weak traceability, and poor visibility of inventory movement. Since Maramag Fish Landing operates in a fast-moving environment where fish boxes, payments, and daily transactions must be monitored accurately, the reviewed literature helps establish the relevance of developing a system tailored to its operational setting.

### 2. Thematic Review of Related Literature

#### Digital Inventory and POS Systems in Small Business Operations

Inventory management and POS systems play a major role in improving business operations, especially in environments where rapid transaction handling and stock visibility are important. Tian and Wang (2022) found that IT capability has a positive effect on inventory strategy and inventory operation process, while also reducing out-of-stock levels. Their findings support the view that digital systems do not merely automate record-keeping; they also improve actual inventory performance.

In a broader SME context, Yuwono et al. (2024) explained that ICT adoption among small and medium-sized enterprises continues to grow, but many SMEs still face barriers such as limited financial resources, lack of ICT knowledge, and reluctance to change. This is relevant to Maramag Fish Landing because small-scale trading environments often need affordable, easy-to-use systems rather than highly complex enterprise platforms.

Muchaendepi et al. (2019) further noted that SMEs that still rely on non-computerized methods experience communication delays, stock-related inefficiencies, and difficulty in maintaining consistent supply visibility. This supports the need for a web-based system in settings where manual tracking is still dominant and where operational delays directly affect daily transactions.

#### QR Codes and Traceability in Food and Fish Supply Chains

Traceability is a critical issue in food and fish supply chains because it improves transparency, accountability, and response capability in case of product problems or inventory disputes. Oliveira et al. (2021) described a traceability platform for the fishery and aquaculture value chain that allowed fish and fishery products to be tracked from source to market through a digital platform. Their work emphasized that digital traceability improves recall capability, information access, and quality monitoring across the value chain.

Parreño-Marchante et al. (2014) also showed that electronic traceability systems in aquaculture can significantly reduce processing time and improve supply chain efficiency. Their study is important because it demonstrates that digital traceability is useful even in fish-related industries where products are perishable and operational timing is critical.

For QR-specific applications, Li et al. (2024) explained that QR codes have become highly suitable for food information transmission because of their large data storage capacity, fast accessibility, and practical role in traceability. Their review highlighted that QR codes are widely used in food traceability, anti-counterfeiting, and information sharing, making them suitable for affordable operational systems.

Jose et al. (2024) proposed a QR code-based traceability system for the dry fish supply chain of micro and small enterprises in India. Their study showed that QR code integration can provide real-time access to information and improve quality and safety monitoring in fish-related small enterprise settings. This is highly relevant to Maramag Fish Landing because it demonstrates that QR-based solutions are feasible in resource-conscious fish product environments.

In addition, Rodriguez-Salvador and Calvo-Dopico (2020) found that consumers value traceability in fishery products because it helps them understand product origin, verify producer claims, and improve confidence in food quality and safety. Although the current study does not focus on consumer-facing traceability, this literature still supports the importance of transparent tracking in fish-related operations.

#### Wet Markets, Local Trading Environments, and Operational Monitoring

Local market systems have unique characteristics that make digitalization both important and challenging. Carmen et al. (2023) found that public wet markets in Iloilo City remain highly important to the local economy, but many vendors still rely on manual accounting systems, do not regularly prepare financial reports, and face limitations in formal operational management. This finding strongly supports the need for tailored digital systems in Philippine wet-market-like environments.

APEC (2024) also emphasized that fisheries traceability systems in Asia-Pacific economies remain uneven, particularly among small-scale operations that face technological and infrastructure constraints. The report pointed out that the digital divide remains a major issue in implementing effective traceability systems in fisheries. This is relevant to the present study because Maramag Fish Landing operates in a local trading environment where affordability and practicality are essential system design considerations.

### 3. Past Studies

#### i. Local Studies

Carmen et al. (2023), in their study on the public wet market system of Iloilo City, found that many wet market enterprises still use manual accounting systems and do not consistently prepare financial reports. Their study highlighted that traditional market operations remain vulnerable to weak documentation and limited formal monitoring. This is closely related to the setting of Maramag Fish Landing, where manual recording also creates operational inefficiencies.

Mangmang (2018) developed and implemented a POS system for a retail business and found that the developed software improved monitoring of overdue payments and out-of-stock items. The study concluded that technological adoption can improve business operations, particularly in environments where monitoring and reporting are essential. This supports the use of POS systems not only for selling but also for better transaction and payment control.

Dalida et al. (2023) developed a POS and inventory system for Ticar-Nuñez Supermart and reported that the system was highly acceptable based on ISO 25010 criteria. Their study showed that digital transaction and inventory systems can improve the monitoring of stock movement and support the generation of accurate reports. This local evidence supports the acceptability of similar systems in practical business settings.

Valencia et al. (2023) developed an inventory system for a drug store in Capiz and incorporated barcode-based functionality together with a POS component that could generate daily, monthly, and yearly sales reports. Their study is relevant because it demonstrates that even in local business settings, code-based identification and POS-supported reporting can strengthen stock monitoring and transaction control.

Bastan et al. (2004) developed a computerized sales and inventory system for a satellite canteen and found that the proposed system could fully monitor transactions, improve report generation, support barcode-based retrieval, and apply user authentication with different access levels. Although the setting differs from a fish landing, the study is still useful because it shows that role-based access and coded item identification can improve operational efficiency.

Magbanua et al. (2007) proposed an automated POS system for Mang Inasal and reported that automation could improve transaction speed, reduce manual errors, and simplify receipt and report preparation. Their study also used the Waterfall model, making it useful as a local example of sequential software development in business transaction systems.

#### ii. International Studies

Parreño-Marchante et al. (2014) developed an advanced traceability system in the aquaculture supply chain by combining digital traceability and environmental monitoring technologies. Their study demonstrated major time reductions in traceability-related processes and showed that electronic traceability can improve efficiency even in fish-related SMEs.

Oliveira et al. (2021) designed a traceability system for quality monitoring in the fishery and aquaculture value chain. The study emphasized the usefulness of a centralized digital platform for improving monitoring, recall capability, and access to fish product traceability information. This study is closely related to the proposed system because it supports the value of structured digital monitoring in fisheries.

Rodriguez-Salvador and Calvo-Dopico (2020) examined fishery product traceability from a consumer perspective and found that consumers associate traceability with origin, process transparency, safety, and accountability. Their findings suggest that traceability is not only operationally valuable but also important in building trust in fish-related value chains.

Jose et al. (2024) proposed a QR code-based traceability system for the dry fish supply chain of micro and small enterprises in India. The study showed that QR code technology can be adopted in fish-related small-enterprise environments to support information access, tracking, and quality management. This directly supports the practicality of QR code use in the present study.

Li et al. (2024) reviewed the use of QR codes in food information systems and concluded that QR codes perform well in traceability, information access, and supply chain integrity. Their work supports the use of QR codes as a practical and efficient digital identification mechanism for food-related operations.

Tian and Wang (2022) found that stronger IT capability improves inventory management performance and lowers out-of-stock levels. Their findings support the inclusion of integrated inventory features in systems that must manage time-sensitive and frequently changing stock conditions.

Yuwono et al. (2024), through a systematic literature review, found that SMEs continue to adopt ICT tools, but implementation is often constrained by resource limitations and capability gaps. This supports the need to design systems that are not only functional but also simple and realistic for local operational settings.

Muchaendepi et al. (2019) found that SMEs without computerized inventory systems often struggle with delays, poor supply visibility, and inefficient communication. Their study provides further support for replacing manual inventory recording with structured digital systems.

### 4. Methods Used

The reviewed studies applied different methods depending on the nature of their research problem and system objectives. Local development-oriented studies such as Mangmang (2018), Dalida et al. (2023), and Valencia et al. (2023) generally used developmental or evaluative approaches in designing and assessing POS and inventory systems. These studies often relied on user evaluation instruments, acceptability testing, and structured questionnaires.

Local contextual studies such as Carmen et al. (2023) used descriptive design and interviews to examine the operational condition of wet markets. This is useful for the present study because it shows that local market conditions are often best understood through direct observation and respondent-based data collection.

International studies on inventory and ICT, such as Tian and Wang (2022) and Yuwono et al. (2024), used empirical analysis and systematic review methods to assess the role of technology in inventory and SME operations. On the other hand, fishery and traceability studies such as Oliveira et al. (2021), Parreño-Marchante et al. (2014), and Jose et al. (2024) focused on system architecture, platform development, applied testing, and evaluation in real or semi-real operational settings.

Overall, the reviewed literature shows that system development studies commonly combine requirement gathering, implementation, and user-based or performance-based evaluation. This supports the present study's use of developmental research, interviews, observation, and User Acceptance Testing.

### 5. Findings and Gaps

The reviewed literature consistently shows that digital systems improve transaction monitoring, inventory visibility, and operational efficiency. Studies on POS and inventory systems indicate that automation reduces delays, improves record accuracy, and supports better reporting. Studies on traceability further show that digital identification methods such as QR codes improve accountability and transparency in food and fish supply chains.

However, several gaps remain.

First, many POS and inventory studies focus on retail, restaurants, warehouses, or supermarkets rather than community-based fish landing operations. While these systems are relevant, they do not fully address the operational realities of fish landing environments where reusable fish boxes, buyer balances, and return accountability are central concerns.

Second, many advanced fishery traceability studies use architectures involving RFID, sensors, or other more complex technologies. Although effective, these approaches may be too costly or impractical for smaller local trading environments. This creates a gap for affordable, web-based, and QR-supported systems designed for small-scale fishery operations.

Third, local studies confirm that many Philippine market environments still depend on manual processes. Yet there remains limited documented research focused specifically on integrating POS, inventory, fish box tracking, and administrative oversight in a fish landing context such as Maramag Fish Landing.

### 6. Synthesis and Critical Evaluation

The reviewed literature provides strong support for the development of the proposed system. Studies on POS and inventory systems show that digitalization improves transaction handling, stock monitoring, and report generation. Studies on ICT in SMEs further show that integrated systems can improve business performance when designed in a practical and context-appropriate way.

Studies on fishery and food traceability demonstrate that digital identification and traceability systems are valuable in improving transparency, quality monitoring, and accountability. QR code-based approaches, in particular, are relevant because they are more affordable and easier to deploy than more advanced alternatives such as RFID- or blockchain-heavy systems.

At the same time, the literature also shows that implementation barriers remain important. SMEs and local trading environments often face financial, technical, and organizational constraints. This means that the most appropriate system for Maramag Fish Landing is not necessarily the most technologically advanced one, but the one that best fits its operational reality.

From a critical standpoint, many existing studies provide useful models, but only a few are directly tailored to fish landing operations involving brokers, reusable containers, balance monitoring, and local government oversight. For this reason, the current study contributes by adapting digital POS and inventory principles to a very specific local operational context.

### 7. Link to the Current Study

The present study is directly supported by the reviewed literature in several ways. First, the literature on POS and inventory systems confirms that automation improves transaction accuracy, stock monitoring, and reporting efficiency. Second, the literature on QR code-based traceability supports the use of affordable digital identification for inventory and fish box tracking. Third, the literature on SMEs and wet markets shows that local trading environments need systems that are both practical and easy to adopt.

The current study addresses the gaps identified in the literature by developing a web-based Point-of-Sale and Inventory Management System specifically for Maramag Fish Landing. Unlike generic retail systems, the proposed system includes features aligned with its operational setting, such as fish type and fish price management, QR-based fish box identification, buyer recording, payment monitoring, receipt printing, broker analytics, and tracking of returned and missing fish boxes.

In this way, the study extends existing literature by providing a localized and operationally grounded system design for a community-based fish landing environment.

In conclusion, the reviewed literature and studies show that digital POS, inventory, and traceability systems can significantly improve efficiency, accuracy, and transparency. However, the literature also reveals a lack of affordable and context-specific systems designed for local fish landing operations. This gap justifies the development of the proposed Point-of-Sale and Inventory Management System for Maramag Fish Landing.
