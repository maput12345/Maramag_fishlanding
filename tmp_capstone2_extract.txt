CHAPTER I: INTRODUCTION
1. Background of the Study
Sales tracking and inventory management are crucial to businesses dealing with perishable products like fish. Manual documentation in Maramag Fish Landing has caused inefficiencies like missing boxes of fish, missing or incomplete sales records, and tracking challenges in everyday transactions. Such inefficiencies reduce transparency, undermine accountability, and affect operational efficiency. Monetary and administrative losses are thereby incurred for fish brokers and the Local Economic Enterprise Office (LEEO), which enables market transactions. Literature shows that manual stock systems are prone to errors and lack of efficiency, leading to financial loss and slowing down processes (Kumar & Suresh, 2021).
Inventory control at Maramag Fish Landing uses handwritten records and manual data input for the inventory management process. This method is, however, very vulnerable to human error. Incomplete records commonly result in disagreements between brokers and buyers, causing the operations to slow down and financial inaccuracies. As per Smith et al. (2022), manual procedures of inventory often lead to inconsistencies in data, which complicates accurate reconciliation of records. In addition, monitoring fish box movements between buyers and brokers is still an issue without a systematic monitoring mechanism, compounding inefficiencies further (Rodriguez & Tan, 2023).
To overcome these challenges, having an inventory management system in place is crucial. A process-based approach helps ensure that all inventory transactions have a uniform workflow, minimizing inconsistencies (Brown & Williams, 2021). An orderly system captures and monitors each transaction, reflecting up-to-date reports on stock positions, sales, and financial information (Garcia & Lopez, 2022). Accuracy improves through automation, minimizing human errors in calculations and documentation (Davis, 2023). Lastly, transparency enables stakeholders to track all transactions, reducing discrepancies and enhancing accountability (Nguyen & Patel, 2023). This research seeks to develop and design a POS and inventory system to enhance efficiency, accuracy, and transparency in Maramag Fish Landing day-to-day operations. With the modernization of digital technologies, companies across the globe are embracing automated inventory and sales management systems. An optimally integrated system allows data processing and automation to maximize business functions (Lee et al., 2021).
Inventory and POS systems have been found to be effective in enhancing transaction accuracy, minimizing errors, and supporting better decision-making (Gonzalez & Martinez, 2022). By adopting a POS and inventory system, Maramag Fish Landing can enhance process efficiency, minimize operational inefficiencies, and provide more reliable and transparent market transactions.
2. Statement of the Problem
Maramag Fish Landing does not yet have an automated inventory and sales tracking system in place, resulting in enormous operational inefficiencies. The main problems lie in the dependence on manual recording of transactions by brokers, the following main problems arising therefrom:
First, the implementation of inventory management system for brokers and LEEO relied on manual recording transactions. A manual process often results in lost or missed records, complicating the process, and slightly lowering accountability, since this method has a high volume of buyers daily.
Second, manual input of sales data can cause lots of errors since the workers are dealing with a busy environment whereby too many transactions occur daily. These circumstances multiply the probability of the human error entering a wrong amount or missing the sale note. Consequently, the sales figures become unreliable, making it difficult for the business to develop valid financial reports or determine its actual financial performance.
Third, there is no system yet to monitor who bought the fish and how many were sold daily. These boxes are costly and have a major determining factor in brokers' revenues as their pay relies on the number of boxes sold per day. But since there is no monitoring, brokers always experience cases of missing or unaccounted boxes. When this occurs, they cannot determine whether the buyer or get back the missing boxes and therefore treat the boxes as lost. Consequently, such missing boxes are regarded as lost, and brokers are compelled to buy new ones further, lowering their daily earnings and instigating a shortage of transparency and accountability in the fish landing operations. Based on a survey of the eight brokers at Maramag Fish Landing, these tracking problems are common and a cause for concern.
If these inefficiencies are not addressed, brokers will struggle to track sales accurately, prepare correct financial reports, and maintain proper inventory levels. To resolve these issues, this research proposes developing a POS and inventory management system that automates processes, enhances transparency, and provides data analysis for tracking fish boxes and tracing sales transactions.
3. Objectives of the Study
General Objective:This study aims to design and implement a POS and inventory management system for Maramag Fish Landing to enhance efficiency, accuracy, and transparency in fish box tracking and sales recording.
Specific Objectives:
Conduct a need assessment to determine primary challenges related to loss of inventory, sales tracking, and record management.
To design, develop and implement a centralized system of sales and management supported by structured databases.
To design, develop and implement a tracking system for fish boxesusing unique identifiers and QR code.
To design, develop and implement a dashboard feature for brokers and LEEO administrators to monitor sales activity.
To design and develop automated reports and analytical insights.
Scope and Limitations
Scope of the Study
This study focuses on the development and implementation of a web-based Point-of-Sale (POS) and Inventory Management System for Maramag Fish Landing. The system is designed to support the daily sales and inventory operations of brokers and staff by digitizing transaction recording, fish box tracking, and report generation.
Each fish box will be assigned a unique identifier represented by a QR code. When scanned using a mobile device or web browser, the code will display relevant details such as the buyer’s name, quantity, and type of fish associated with the box. This feature aims to improve accuracy in monitoring fish box distribution, returns, and status.
The system will be accessible online, allowing authorized users to obtain real-time updates of sales and inventory records. Data security will be maintained through user authentication and privilege-based access control, where each user is assigned specific permissions according to their role. Only verified users will be allowed to access sensitive information, which helps prevent unauthorized access and ensures proper handling of data.
The system will assist brokers and staff in recording sales transactions, managing buyer information, monitoring outstanding balances, and tracking fish boxes that have not yet been returned. It will also generate daily sales reports and inventory summaries to support operational monitoring.
Administrative access will be granted to the Local Economic Enterprise Office (LEEO) of Maramag. As system administrators, LEEO personnel will be able to view consolidated sales records, monitor broker activities, and track the number of fish boxes sold or distributed by each broker. The system will include a dashboard that presents key information such as sales performance, inventory status, and basic analytics (e.g., most sold fish per day).
The system is intended exclusively for Maramag Fish Landing and is designed to support the fast-paced trading environment of the area. Buyers will not have direct access to the system; their information will be recorded by brokers during transactions.
Limitations of the Study
The system is tailored specifically to the operational structure, workflow, and requirements of Maramag Fish Landing. As such, it may not be directly applicable to other fish markets, companies, or organizations with different processes or business models without further modification.
The application focuses on essential operational functions such as sales recording, inventory management, and fish box tracking. Advanced features such as demand forecasting, artificial intelligence–based analytics, or predictive decision-making tools are beyond the scope of this study.
The system requires a stable internet connection to provide real-time synchronization of data. Its performance may be affected in areas with unreliable connectivity. Additionally, only authorized brokers, staff, and LEEO administrators will be allowed to access the system, while buyers will have no direct interaction with it.
5. Significance of the Study
This study is significant as it developed a Point-of-Sale (POS) and Inventory Management System that improved the operational efficiency of Maramag Fish Landing. By automating essential processes such as transaction recording, fish box tracking, real-time inventory updating, and report generation, the system enhanced accuracy, transparency, and efficiency in daily operations while reducing manual errors and disputes.
For brokers and staff, the system simplified sales recording, inventory monitoring, and fish box management. It reduced reliance on manual methods, minimized human error, and improved the speed of transactions, enabling personnel to handle high-volume trading more effectively.
For the Local Economic Enterprise Office (LEEO) of Maramag, the system provided centralized access to sales data, broker activities, and inventory information. This supported better supervision, data-driven decision-making, and improved monitoring of fish landing operations.
For the buyers, although they did not directly access the system, they benefited indirectly through faster transactions, more accurate records, and improved accountability in fish box tracking and balance monitoring.
For future researchers and system developers, this study serves as a reference for designing digital solutions for community-based markets and industries dealing with perishable goods. It demonstrates how information technology can be applied to address operational inefficiencies in a fast-paced trading environment.

2. Thematic Review of Related Literature
i. Digital Inventory and POS Systems in Small Business Operations
Inventory management and POS systems play a major role in improving business operations, especially in environments where rapid transaction handling and stock visibility are important. Tian and Wang (2022) found that IT capability has a positive effect on inventory strategy and inventory operation process, while also reducing out-of-stock levels. Their findings support the view that digital systems do not merely automate record-keeping; they also improve actual inventory performance.
In a broader SME context, Yuwono et al. (2024) explained that ICT adoption among small and medium-sized enterprises continues to grow, but many SMEs still face barriers such as limited financial resources, lack of ICT knowledge, and reluctance to change. This is relevant to Maramag Fish Landing because small-scale trading environments often need affordable, easy-to-use systems rather than highly complex enterprise platforms.
Muchaendepi et al. (2019) further noted that SMEs that still rely on non-computerized methods experience communication delays, stock-related inefficiencies, and difficulty in maintaining consistent supply visibility. This supports the need for a web-based system in settings where manual tracking is still dominant and where operational delays directly affect daily transactions.
ii. QR Codes and Traceability in Food and Fish Supply Chains
Traceability is a critical issue in food and fish supply chains because it improves transparency, accountability, and response capability in case of product problems or inventory disputes. Oliveira et al. (2021) described a traceability platform for the fishery and aquaculture value chain that allowed fish and fishery products to be tracked from source to market through a digital platform. Their work emphasized that digital traceability improves recall capability, information access, and quality monitoring across the value chain.
Parreño-Marchante et al. (2014) also showed that electronic traceability systems in aquaculture can significantly reduce processing time and improve supply chain efficiency. Their study is important because it demonstrates that digital traceability is useful even in fish-related industries where products are perishable and operational timing is critical.
For QR-specific applications, Li et al. (2024) explained that QR codes have become highly suitable for food information transmission because of their large data storage capacity, fast accessibility, and practical role in traceability. Their review highlighted that QR codes are widely used in food traceability, anti-counterfeiting, and information sharing, making them suitable for affordable operational systems.
Jose et al. (2024) proposed a QR code-based traceability system for the dry fish supply chain of micro and small enterprises in India. Their study showed that QR code integration can provide real-time access to information and improve quality and safety monitoring in fish-related small enterprise settings. This is highly relevant to Maramag Fish Landing because it demonstrates that QR-based solutions are feasible in resource-conscious fish product environments.
In addition, Rodriguez-Salvador and Calvo-Dopico (2020) found that consumers value traceability in fishery products because it helps them understand product origin, verify producer claims, and improve confidence in food quality and safety. Although the current study does not focus on consumer-facing traceability, this literature still supports the importance of transparent tracking in fish-related operations.
Wet Markets, Local Trading Environments, and Operational Monitoring
Local market systems have unique characteristics that make digitalization both important and challenging. Carmen et al. (2023) found that public wet markets in Iloilo City remain highly important to the local economy, but many vendors still rely on manual accounting systems, do not regularly prepare financial reports, and face limitations in formal operational management. APEC (2024) also emphasized that fisheries traceability systems in Asia-Pacific economies remain uneven, particularly among small-scale operations that face technological and infrastructure constraints. The report pointed out that the digital divide remains a major issue in implementing effective traceability systems in fisheries. This is relevant to the present study because Maramag Fish Landing operates in a local trading environment where affordability and practicality are essential system design considerations.

5. Findings and Gaps
The reviewed literature consistently shows that digital systems improve transaction monitoring, inventory visibility, and operational efficiency. Studies on traceability further show that digital identification methods such as QR codes improve accountability and transparency in food and fish supply chains.
However, several gaps remain.
First, many POS and inventory studies focus on retail, restaurants, warehouses, or supermarkets rather than community-based fish landing operations. While these systems are relevant, they do not fully address the operational realities of fish landing environments where reusable fish boxes, buyer balances, and return accountability are central concerns.
Second, many advanced fishery traceability studies use architectures involving RFID, sensors, or other more complex technologies. Although effective, these approaches may be too costly or impractical for smaller local trading environments. This creates a gap for affordable, web-based, and QR-supported systems designed for small-scale fishery operations.
Third, local studies confirm that many Philippine market environments still depend on manual processes. Yet there remains limited documented research focused specifically on integrating POS, inventory, fish box tracking, and administrative oversight in a fish landing context such as Maramag Fish Landing.

The reviewed literature provides strong support for the development of the proposed system. From a critical standpoint, many existing studies provide useful models, but only a few are directly tailored to fish landing operations involving brokers, reusable containers, balance monitoring, and local government oversight. For this reason, the current study contributes by adapting digital POS and inventory principles to a very specific local operational context.

CHAPTER III: TECHNICAL BACKGROUND
1. Overview of the System
The proposed project is a web-based Point-of-Sale (POS) and Inventory Management System developed for Maramag Fish Landing. The system was designed to address the problems observed in the existing manual process, particularly in sales recording, fish box tracking, payment monitoring, and report preparation. Under the manual setup, brokers and administrative personnel relied on handwritten records and manual checking of transactions, which made the process time-consuming, prone to error, and difficult to monitor consistently.
The developed system provides a centralized digital platform for recording sales transactions, managing fish-related inventory information, monitoring customer payments, and tracking fish boxes through QR codes. It supports faster transaction processing, better record organization, and improved transparency in daily operations. The system is intended for two major user groups: broker users and authorized administrative users under the Local Economic Enterprise Office (LEEO), specifically admin and staff accounts.
For broker users, the system provides modules for fish type management, fish price management, fish box registration, QR-based fish box identification, sales recording, payment entry, receipt printing, and fish box tracking. For administrative users, the system provides centralized monitoring of users, broker activities, sales summaries, and fish box tracking records. Through these modules, the system helps improve efficiency, accuracy, and accountability in Maramag Fish Landing operations.
2. Technologies Involved
i. Back End
The system was developed using PHP and the Laravel framework. Laravel was selected because it provides a structured and maintainable framework for developing secure web applications. It follows the Model-View-Controller (MVC) architectural pattern, which separates the business logic, presentation layer, and data access layer. This structure made the system easier to organize, maintain, and expand during development.
MySQL was used as the database management system for storing user information, broker profiles, buyer records, fish types, fish prices, fish boxes, sales transactions, payments, and fish box movement logs. Laravel migrations were used to define and manage the database structure, while Eloquent ORM was used to simplify database operations and model relationships.
XAMPP was used as the local development environment to run Apache and MySQL during development and testing. Composer was used to install and manage PHP package dependencies required by the Laravel application. The back-end implementation also used Laravel authentication, validation, middleware, route grouping, and CSRF protection to support secure access and transaction handling.
ii. Front End
The user interface of the system was developed using Laravel Blade templates, Tailwind CSS, HTML, CSS, and JavaScript. Blade was used as the server-side templating engine for constructing reusable page layouts and dynamic views. Tailwind CSS was used as the main styling framework for building a responsive and clean user interface suitable for desktop and mobile browser use.
Alpine.js was used in the interface for lightweight reactive behavior, such as modal state handling, form interaction, sidebar control, and filtering components. JavaScript was used to support client-side interaction and dynamic processing. Axios was included for HTTP requests, while SweetAlert2 and Toastr were used for user notifications and confirmation dialogs.
For QR-related functionality, the system used QR code generation and QR scanning libraries to support fish box identification and return processing. Laravel Mix and Node Package Manager (NPM) were used to compile and bundle front-end assets such as JavaScript and CSS files.
3. Architecture and Design Concepts
The system follows a client-server architecture in which the client side consists of browser-based user interfaces and the server side processes requests, applies business logic, and stores data in the database. Users interact with the system through a web browser, while the application server processes transactions and retrieves the necessary data from the MySQL database.
The implementation follows the Model-View-Controller (MVC) architecture. The models represent the application data and database relationships, the views provide the user interface, and the controllers handle user requests and system logic. This architectural approach was suitable for the project because it improved code organization and allowed the developers to manage the system in modular form.
The system also uses role-based access control. Broker users are allowed to access modules related to inventory, pricing, sales, payments, and fish box tracking. Administrative users under the LEEO office, specifically admin and staff accounts, are allowed to access account management, centralized monitoring, sales analysis, and fish box tracking views. This design helps protect sensitive information and ensures that each user can only access the features relevant to their role.
4. Existing Solutions or Benchmarks
Existing Point-of-Sale and inventory management systems are commonly used in retail and service-based businesses. These systems generally support transaction recording, inventory monitoring, and report generation. However, most available solutions are designed for general retail environments and do not specifically address the operational workflow of a fish landing facility that relies on reusable fish boxes, box-return monitoring, and broker-based transaction handling.
The developed system differs from generic POS applications because it was customized for the operational setting of Maramag Fish Landing. In addition to sales recording, it supports fish type management, fish price management, fish box registration, QR-based identification, partial payment monitoring, fish box return processing, and missing box monitoring. These functions directly support the needs of brokers and LEEO administrative users in the local fish landing environment.
The system was designed as a practical and low-cost web-based solution. Instead of using more complex technologies such as RFID, IoT, or blockchain, the project uses QR code identification and browser-based access to achieve a balance between usability, affordability, and operational effectiveness.
CHAPTER IV: Methodology
1. Conceptual Framework
Figure 1. Conceptual Framework
In Figure 1, the data flow and interaction among the primary actors and system components involved in the Point-of-Sale (POS) and Inventory System for Maramag Fish Landing are presented. The framework highlights the roles of the Customer, Broker/Staff, Broker Computer, QR-coded fish boxes, Web Server, Database, and the Local Economic Enterprise Office (LEEO).
The process begins when a customer places an order with the broker or staff. The order details are received and encoded into the Broker Computer through the POS interface operated by the broker or staff. During the transaction, QR codes attached to fish boxes are scanned to uniquely identify each box associated with the customer’s order. This scanning mechanism ensures accurate identification and proper tracking of fish boxes, minimizing errors and duplication.
Once the order and corresponding box information are recorded, the Broker Computer transmits the data to the Web Server through HTTP requests. The Web Server processes the transaction by applying the system’s business logic, validating the data, computing totals, updating order status, and preparing the information for storage.
The Web Server then interacts with the Database, where transaction records, fish box records, and inventory data are securely stored. This centralized database ensures data integrity, consistency, and long-term record keeping. After the data is successfully saved, the Web Server sends updated information back to the Broker Computer. The system then generates a receipt for the customer, confirming that the order has been served. At the same time, inventory records are automatically updated in real time to reflect the completed transaction.
Furthermore, the fish box records stored in the database are accessible to the LEEO Computer for administrative monitoring and verification purposes. This allows the Local Economic Enterprise Office (LEEO) to supervise transactions, review reports, and ensure transparency in fish landing operations.
2. Research Approach
This study employed a mixed-method developmental research design. Qualitative methods were used during the requirement gathering stage, while quantitative evaluation was applied after system development through User Acceptance Testing (UAT).
The qualitative component consisted of interviews and direct observation. Interviews were conducted to gather information from intended users regarding the difficulties they encountered in manual sales recording, fish box accountability, and payment monitoring. Direct observation was also used to examine the actual workflow at Maramag Fish Landing, including how sales were recorded, how fish boxes were monitored, and how payment records were handled under the manual system. These methods helped identify the operational problems that guided the development of the proposed system.
The quantitative component consisted of User Acceptance Testing using a structured evaluation instrument based on a 5-point Likert scale. The developed system was evaluated in terms of functionality, usability, accuracy, efficiency, and overall satisfaction. This research approach was appropriate because the study involved both the development of a software solution and the measurement of its acceptability among intended users.
3. Software Development Model
Figure 2. Waterfall Methodology
The development of the system followed the Waterfall Model. The Waterfall Model was used because it provided a clear and organized sequence of activities from requirement gathering up to testing and deployment. Since the study aimed to build a specific system based on identified operational problems, a sequential development model was appropriate for documenting the project from analysis to implementation.
During the requirements phase, the researchers conducted interviews and direct observation to identify the limitations of the existing manual process in Maramag Fish Landing. The main concerns identified included inconsistent sales recording, difficulty in identifying fish boxes, weak tracking of returned and missing boxes, and challenges in monitoring buyer balances and payments. From these findings, the functional and non-functional requirements of the system were defined.
The requirements gathered during this phase led to the identification of the major modules of the system, namely user and access management, fish type management, fish price management, fish box registration, sales recording, payment monitoring, receipt printing, analytics, and administrative monitoring. Ethical considerations such as proper data handling and confidentiality of user information were observed during the information-gathering process.
During the design phase, the structure of the system was planned based on the gathered requirements. The researchers developed the conceptual framework, use case diagram, entity relationship diagram, data flow diagram, and user interface structure. The database design was organized into related tables to support users, roles, employees, brokers, buyers, fish types, broker fish type assignments, fish prices, fish boxes, fish box purchase cycles, fish inventory logs, sales transactions, sales details, and payments.
The interface design was organized according to user roles. Broker views were designed for operational tasks such as fish box management, sales recording, payment entry, and QR scanning. Administrative views were designed for account management, dashboard reporting, broker monitoring, and fish box tracking. Role-based access and validation rules were incorporated into the design to help secure the application.
During the implementation phase, the system was built as a Laravel web application. The local development environment was prepared using XAMPP, Composer, Node Package Manager (NPM), and MySQL. The application environment was configured through the `.env` file, after which Laravel migrations were executed to create the database tables needed by the system.
The development process included the following implementation activities:
1. Configuration of the Laravel project environment and database connection.
2. Creation of database migrations for the main system tables.
3. Development of Eloquent models and model relationships.
4. Implementation of authentication and role-based middleware.
5. Development of broker-side modules for fish types, fish prices, fish boxes, sales, payments, analytics, and receipt printing.
6. Development of administrative modules for dashboards, user management, broker sales analysis, and fish box tracking.
7. Integration of QR code generation and QR scanning for fish box identification and return processing.
8. Compilation of front-end assets through Laravel Mix.
Through this phase, the researchers translated the planned design into a functioning web-based system.
During the testing phase, the researchers examined the developed system through unit testing, integration testing, system testing, and User Acceptance Testing. 5. System Requirements Specification
i. Functional Requirements
The functional requirements of the system describe the main services and features that must be provided to its users.
For broker users, the system must allow login and authenticated access, profile viewing and updating, management of fish types, recording of fish prices, registration and updating of fish boxes, QR-based identification of fish boxes, creation and updating of sales transactions, recording of buyer information, recording of payments, computation of remaining balances, receipt printing, and monitoring of fish box status.
For administrative users, the system must allow login and authenticated access, management of broker and admin or staff accounts, viewing of dashboard summaries, broker sales analysis, and monitoring of fish box movement records, particularly returned and missing fish boxes.
The system must also generate reports and summary information relevant to daily operations, including sales totals, fish box counts, outstanding balances, and fish box tracking records.
ii. Non-Functional Requirements
The non-functional requirements define the quality characteristics of the system.
In terms of security, the system must require authenticated login before user access is granted. It must enforce role-based access control so that broker users and administrative users only access the modules appropriate to their assigned roles. Input validation and protected request handling must be applied to minimize invalid or unauthorized transactions.
In terms of usability, the system must provide a responsive and understandable user interface that can be used by intended users with minimal difficulty. The interface must be suitable for both desktop and mobile browser access, especially for modules that use QR code scanning.
In terms of performance, the system must be capable of processing routine operational transactions such as sales entry, payment recording, and fish box updates within a reasonable time. In terms of maintainability, the system must be organized in modular form using Laravel MVC to support easier future updates and improvement.
iii. Input Requirements
The system requires several categories of input from users. For authentication, users must enter valid login credentials such as email and password. For inventory-related activities, broker users must enter fish type information, fish price information, fish box details, and QR-based fish box inputs.
For transaction processing, broker users must enter sales date, buyer name, optional buyer contact number, selected fish boxes, unit prices, subtotals, and payment information. For account management, administrative users must enter user profile information such as name, email, contact number, role, address, stall name, and position, depending on the account type being created or updated.
These input requirements support the system's operation in managing accounts, transactions, inventory, and monitoring functions.
iv. Output Requirements
The system produces several outputs needed for operational and administrative monitoring. These include dashboard summaries, sales records, payment summaries, remaining balance information, fish box status summaries, fish box tracking records, and printed receipts.
For broker users, the outputs include recent sales, daily sales summaries, outstanding balance information, payment history, and printed transaction receipts. For administrative users, the outputs include centralized dashboard summaries, broker sales analysis, top broker and fish type information, and records of returned and missing fish boxes.
These outputs support transaction confirmation, record review, and management decision-making within Maramag Fish Landing.
v. Software Requirements
The software requirements of the system include a web server environment capable of running PHP and Laravel, a MySQL database server, and a modern web browser. The application was developed and tested using XAMPP as the local web server package, Composer for PHP dependency management, Node Package Manager for front-end dependencies, and Laravel Mix for compiling JavaScript and CSS assets.
The system also requires browser support for JavaScript and camera access for QR scanning features. It may be accessed through browsers such as Google Chrome, Microsoft Edge, Mozilla Firefox, and other browser applications compatible with modern web standards.

Fish box movement history is recorded in the `fish_inventory` table, which stores the status changes of fish boxes such as In Stock, Sold, Returned, and Missing.
This relational structure allows the system to manage users, buyers, fish inventory, pricing, sales transactions, payments, and fish box tracking in an organized and consistent manner.

7. Development and Testing: Levels of Software Testing
i. Unit Testing
Unit testing was used to examine individual parts of the system independently. The researchers checked specific components such as login validation, user management logic, fish box registration, sales processing, payment entry, QR scanning response, and fish box status updating. The purpose of this level of testing was to verify that each module performed its intended function correctly before being combined with other modules.
ii. Integration Testing
Integration testing was performed after the individual modules had been verified. At this stage, the interaction between connected components was tested. Examples include the connection between sales transactions and fish box status updates, the connection between payment records and computed sales balance, and the connection between fish box movement updates and inventory tracking logs. This testing confirmed that the integrated modules exchanged data correctly and supported the overall workflow of the application.
iii. System Testing
System testing was conducted to assess the performance of the complete system as one whole application. The researchers tested the combined operation of authentication, inventory management, QR processing, sales entry, payment recording, dashboard reporting, and account management. This testing was used to verify that the application satisfied both its functional and non-functional requirements.
iv. User Acceptance Testing (UAT)User Acceptance Testing (UAT) was conducted to evaluate the system from the perspective of actual end users, including brokers, staff, and LEEO administrators. A total of 8 respondents participated in the evaluation. The respondents were asked to perform real operational tasks such as recording sales transactions, managing inventory, scanning QR codes, and generating reports. The evaluation utilized a 5-point Likert scale to measure system performance in terms of functionality, usability, accuracy, efficiency, and overall satisfaction. Likert scales are widely used to quantify user perceptions and system acceptability (Sullivan & Artino, 2013).
CHAPTER V
RESULT AND DISCUSSION
Presentation of the Developed System
The Point-of-Sale and Inventory Management System for Maramag Fish Landing was successfully developed and implemented as a web-based application. The system was designed to address the operational problems identified during the requirement gathering stage, particularly those related to manual sales recording, inconsistent payment monitoring, difficulty in tracking fish boxes, and delayed preparation of reports.
The developed system provides a centralized platform for broker users and authorized administrative users. For broker users, the application includes modules for fish type management, fish price management, fish box registration, QR code-based fish box identification, sales recording, payment entry, receipt printing, and dashboard analytics. For administrative users under the LEEO office, the system provides monitoring features such as dashboard summaries, broker sales analysis, fish box tracking, and user account management.
The implementation of these modules demonstrates that the system was able to translate the identified requirements into actual operational features. The developed application therefore served as a practical digital solution for improving record organization, transparency, and monitoring efficiency in Maramag Fish Landing.
Results of System Implementation
i. Broker-Side Operational Functions
The broker-side functions of the system were successfully implemented. Broker users were able to manage fish types assigned to them, maintain fish prices, register fish boxes, and use QR codes to identify fish boxes during operational processes. These features addressed the problem of manual and inconsistent encoding of fish-related inventory information.
The fish type and fish price modules made it possible for broker users to maintain organized fish classifications and updated prices inside the system. This improved consistency in transaction processing because fish box records and sales entries could be linked to a defined fish type and corresponding price.
The fish box module also functioned as intended. Brokers were able to create fish box records, update fish box details, and monitor fish box status. Since each fish box was assigned a unique QR code, identification became faster and more accurate than manual checking. This addressed the earlier problem of difficulty in tracking which fish boxes were in stock, sold, returned, or missing.
ii. Sales and Payment Processing Results
The sales module enabled broker users to record sales transactions in a structured manner. During the sales process, the broker could select or scan available fish boxes, encode buyer information, and save the corresponding transaction. The system stored the transaction in the sales records and linked the itemized sale details to the related fish box records. As a result, the process of recording sales became more organized and less prone to manual error.
The payment module also operated successfully. The system allowed broker users to record payment entries associated with a sale and automatically reflect the corresponding paid amount and remaining balance. This improved the monitoring of unpaid and partially paid transactions. Compared with the manual process, the developed payment module provided a more reliable way of tracking balances and reviewing payment history.
Receipt printing was also successfully integrated into the system. After recording a sale and its related payments, the system could generate a printable receipt that displayed transaction details, buyer information, payment information, and the remaining balance. This feature improved transaction documentation and supported better record confirmation during daily operations.
iii. QR Code and Fish Box Tracking Results
One of the major features of the system is the use of QR codes for fish box identification. The implemented QR code functionality supported two main purposes: retrieving fish box information during sales processing and returning fish boxes through QR-based scanning. This reduced the need for manual fish box identification and helped speed up operational tasks.
The fish box tracking mechanism also functioned successfully. When fish boxes changed status, such as from In Stock to Sold, Returned, or Missing, the corresponding updates were stored in the tracking records of the system. This improved accountability in fish box movement and allowed users to monitor the current and historical condition of each fish box.
The availability of fish box tracking records was particularly important for administrative monitoring. It enabled authorized users to review the movement of fish boxes and identify issues involving unreturned or missing units. This directly addressed one of the major operational concerns observed during the requirement analysis stage.
iv. Dashboard and Administrative Monitoring Results
The dashboard and monitoring modules were also successfully implemented. For broker users, the dashboard presented summarized operational information such as total sales, outstanding balance, recent sales activity, and trend-based analytics. These outputs provided users with a clearer view of their daily transaction performance and helped improve monitoring of their own operations.
For authorized administrative users, the system provided centralized monitoring features. The administrative dashboard displayed summarized information such as total brokers, total fish boxes sold, returned fish boxes, missing fish boxes, top brokers, and top fish types sold. The administrative module also supported broker-based sales analysis and fish box tracking history. Through these features, the system improved transparency and gave administrative users better access to records needed for monitoring and supervision.
The user management component also contributed to administrative control by allowing authorized personnel to create, update, activate, deactivate, and manage user accounts. This improved account organization and supported role-based system access.

3. Discussion of the Results
The implemented results show that the developed system was able to address the problems identified in the earlier chapters of the study. The manual process previously used in Maramag Fish Landing was characterized by slow recording, weak fish box accountability, and difficulty in monitoring balances and reports. Through system development, these concerns were addressed by providing structured digital modules for sales processing, payment monitoring, fish box tracking, and administrative reporting.
The sales module addressed the problem of incomplete and inconsistent manual sales recording by organizing sales information into structured transaction records. The payment module improved accountability in buyer balances by systematically storing payment records and computing remaining amounts. The fish box module and QR code functionality reduced errors in fish box identification and strengthened monitoring of box status. The dashboards and reports improved operational transparency by providing timely access to summarized information for both brokers and administrative users.
The results therefore indicate that the developed system was not only technically functional but also operationally relevant to the workflow of Maramag Fish Landing. The system features were aligned with the actual needs identified during interviews and observation, which supports the appropriateness of the developed solution.
CHAPTER VI
CONCLUSION AND RECOMMENDATION
1.Conclusion
The creation of the Point-of-Sale (POS) and Inventory Management System on Maramag Fish Landing was made, and it was able to accomplish its targeted goals. The system was developed to overcome the predicament of the previous manual process especially in recording the sales transactions, tracking of fish boxes, inventory monitoring and transparency to the administrative view.
The process of the development was properly planned, analyzed, designed, implemented and tested to make sure that the system is employed as per the requirements of the brokers, staff, and the Local Economic Enterprise Office (LEEO). The system will combine key attributes like user authentication, fish box tracking using QR codes, sales and payments, inventory, generation of a receipt and generation of a report.
The User Acceptance Testing (UAT) was conducted on eight (8) respondents with an overall mean score of 4.70, which is verbally acceptable as Strongly Acceptable. This is a testimony to the fact that the system is efficient, accurate, user-friendly, and functional. The respondents were involved with the performance of the system and stated that it is in good support of the daily activity of Maramag Fish Landing.
According to the outcome and user expectations, the system has been found to be a trusted tool that increases the precision of the transactions, cuts down on the manual load, extends inventory management and facilitates transparency within the fish landing activity.
2. Recommendations
The Point-of-Sale (POS) and Inventory Management System on Maramag Fish Landing has proven to be acceptable and has provided satisfactory results during testing. However, the proponent would like to make the following recommendations to further enhanced the system's performance and user experience:
i. Message Alerts for Remaining Balance:
To improve the overall excellence of the Point-of-Sale (POS) and Inventory Management System on Maramag Fish Landing, it was highly recommended adding a feature that sends message alerts to customers regarding their remaining balance. Since the system collects customers' phone numbers, this functionality will enable the system to send text messages notifying customers about their remaining balance. This served as a helpful reminder for customers to stay informed about their current balance.
ii. Message Alerts for Payment Confirmation:
Another valuable recommendation was to implement a message alert system that notifies customers whenever a payment was received at the counter. This feature will greatly benefit customers by providing them with instant notifications, ensuring that they were aware that their payment has been confirmed and recorded.
By implementing these suggested features, the Point-of-Sale (POS) and Inventory Management System on Maramag Fish Landing was further optimized, enhancing the overall user You sent experience and providing customers with improved convenience and timely updates.
